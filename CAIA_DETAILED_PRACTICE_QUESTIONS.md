### Question 1: Alternative Investments - Liquidity Characteristics

**Topic**: Introduction to Alternative Investments

**Question:**
Which of the following best describes the liquidity characteristics of most alternative investments compared to traditional investments?

A) Alternative investments typically have higher liquidity and more frequent trading than traditional investments  
B) Alternative investments generally have lower liquidity and longer holding periods than traditional investments  
C) Alternative investments have identical liquidity profiles to traditional investments  
D) Liquidity is not a relevant consideration for alternative investments

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation When Correct:**
‚úÖ **Excellent!** You correctly identified that alternative investments generally have lower liquidity than traditional investments. This is a fundamental characteristic that distinguishes alternatives from traditional asset classes like publicly traded stocks and bonds.

**Why This Answer is Correct:**
- Alternative investments (private equity, real estate, hedge funds with lock-ups) typically have longer holding periods
- Many alternatives have lock-up periods, gates, or redemption restrictions
- Private market investments cannot be easily sold on public exchanges
- This illiquidity is often compensated with an "illiquidity premium" in expected returns

**Why Other Answers Are Wrong:**

‚ùå **Option A is incorrect** because alternative investments typically have LOWER liquidity, not higher. Traditional investments like stocks and bonds trade on public exchanges with high liquidity.

‚ùå **Option C is incorrect** because alternatives and traditional investments have very different liquidity profiles. Traditional investments are generally much more liquid.

‚ùå **Option D is incorrect** because liquidity is a critical consideration for alternative investments. Investors must carefully assess their liquidity needs and the liquidity constraints of alternatives before investing.

A) A sign of poor fund management

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation When Correct:**

**Why This Answer is Correct:**

‚ùå **Option D is incorrect** because the J-curve is a legitimate, well-documented phenomenon in private equity. It is not evidence of fraud.

### Question 3: Hedge Funds - Fee Structure

**Topic**: Hedge Funds

**Question:**
A hedge fund charges a "2 and 20" fee structure. If the fund has $100 million in assets under management and generates a 15% return in a year (with a 5% hurdle rate and high-water mark provision), what is the total fee paid to the fund manager?

A) $2 million  
B) $3.5 million  
C) $4 million  
D) $5 million

**Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation When Correct:**
‚úÖ **Correct calculation!** You properly accounted for both the management fee and performance fee.

**Why This Answer is Correct:**
- Management fee: 2% of $100 million = $2 million
- Performance fee calculation:
  - Return: 15% of $100 million = $15 million
  - Hurdle rate: 5% of $100 million = $5 million
  - Excess return above hurdle: $15 million - $5 million = $10 million
  - Performance fee: 20% of $10 million = $2 million
- Total fees: $2 million (management) + $2 million (performance) = $4 million

**Why Other Answers Are Wrong:**

‚ùå **Option A ($2 million)** only accounts for the management fee and ignores the performance fee.

‚ùå **Option B ($3.5 million)** incorrectly calculates the performance fee or doesn't properly account for the hurdle rate.

‚ùå **Option D ($5 million)** overcalculates the fees, possibly by applying the performance fee to the entire return rather than the excess above the hurdle.

### Question 9: Quantitative Methods - Sharpe Ratio

**Topic**: Introduction to Alternative Investments

**Question:**
An investment has an expected return of 12%, a standard deviation of 18%, and the risk-free rate is 3%. What is the Sharpe ratio?

A) 0.50  
B) 0.67  
C) 0.75  
D) 1.00

**Correct Answer: A**

**Explanation When Correct:**
‚úÖ **Correct calculation!** You properly applied the Sharpe ratio formula.

**Why This Answer is Correct:**
- Sharpe Ratio = (Expected Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation
- Sharpe Ratio = (12% - 3%) / 18%
- Sharpe Ratio = 9% / 18% = 0.50

**Why Other Answers Are Wrong:**

‚ùå **Option B (0.67)** would result from incorrectly using 12% / 18% without subtracting the risk-free rate.

‚ùå **Option C (0.75)** would result from using (12% - 3%) / 12% = 9% / 12%.

‚ùå **Option D (1.00)** would result from using 18% / 18% or not accounting for the risk-free rate properly.

### Question 12: Risk Management - Value at Risk

**Topic**: Risk and Risk Management

**Question:**
A portfolio has a Value at Risk (VaR) of $10 million at the 95% confidence level over one day. This means:

A) The portfolio will lose exactly $10 million tomorrow  
B) There is a 5% probability of losing more than $10 million in one day  
C) The portfolio has never lost more than $10 million  
D) The portfolio is guaranteed to lose less than $10 million

**Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation When Correct:**
‚úÖ **Perfect understanding!** You correctly interpreted VaR as a probabilistic measure of potential loss.

**Why This Answer is Correct:**
- VaR at 95% confidence means there is a 5% probability (1 - 0.95) that losses will exceed $10 million
- VaR is a statistical measure, not a guarantee
- It estimates potential loss under normal market conditions
- It does not predict exact losses or guarantee maximum losses
- VaR helps quantify risk but has limitations (doesn't capture tail risk beyond the confidence level)

**Why Other Answers Are Wrong:**

‚ùå **Option A is incorrect** because VaR is a probabilistic estimate, not a prediction of exact losses. The portfolio may lose more, less, or nothing.

‚ùå **Option C is incorrect** because VaR is a forward-looking estimate, not a statement about historical performance.

‚ùå **Option D is incorrect** because VaR does not provide a guarantee. There is a 5% chance losses could exceed $10 million.

### Question 21: Private Equity Valuation and Risks

**Topic**: Methods and Models / Private Equity

**Question:**
**(a)** Explain three methods commonly used to value private equity investments.

**(b)** Discuss two risks specific to private equity investments during periods of economic downturn.

**Sample Answer:**

**(a) Three valuation methods:**

1. **Comparable Company Analysis (Comps)**: Uses valuation multiples (P/E, EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue) from similar publicly traded companies to estimate the value of the private company. Adjustments are made for differences in size, growth, profitability, and market position.

2. **Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis**: Projects future cash flows of the company and discounts them to present value using an appropriate discount rate (typically WACC or cost of equity). Includes terminal value to capture value beyond the projection period.

3. **Precedent Transaction Analysis**: Analyzes purchase prices paid in similar M&A transactions to establish valuation benchmarks. Uses transaction multiples (purchase price / revenue, EBITDA, etc.) from comparable deals.

**(b) Two risks during economic downturns:**

1. **Portfolio Company Distress**: Economic downturns reduce consumer spending, corporate revenues, and access to credit. Portfolio companies may face:
   - Revenue declines and margin compression
   - Difficulty refinancing debt
   - Covenant breaches
   - Potential bankruptcy or restructuring
   This can lead to write-downs or total loss of investments.

2. **Exit Challenges**: Economic downturns reduce M&A activity and IPO markets:
   - Fewer buyers and lower valuations
   - Extended holding periods
   - Reduced exit multiples
   - Difficulty achieving target returns
   This can extend fund life, reduce returns, and create liquidity issues for LPs.

- **LDI**: Considers funding ratio and surplus risk
- **Traditional**: May ignore liability structure entirely
- **LDI**: Uses derivatives (swaps, options) to adjust asset characteristics
- **Traditional**: Primarily uses physical assets

**(b) Implementation Example:**

A defined benefit pension plan with long-duration, inflation-sensitive liabilities might:

1. **Match Duration**: Use long-duration bonds or interest rate swaps to match the duration of pension liabilities, reducing interest rate risk.

2. **Inflation Hedging**: Allocate to inflation-linked bonds (TIPS) or inflation swaps to hedge against inflation risk in cost-of-living adjustments.

3. **Growth Portfolio**: Maintain a growth portfolio (equities, alternatives) for the surplus (assets above liability value) to generate returns while hedging the core liability risk.

4. **Cash Flow Matching**: Structure bond portfolios to match expected benefit payment cash flows.

### Question 23: Hedge Fund Risk Management

**Topic**: Risk and Risk Management / Hedge Funds

**Question:**
**(a)** Explain two key risks that hedge fund investors should monitor.

**(b)** Describe one risk management technique that hedge fund managers can employ to mitigate these risks.

**Sample Answer:**

**(a) Two Key Risks:**

1. **Leverage Risk**: Hedge funds often use significant leverage (borrowed money or derivatives) to amplify returns. High leverage:
   - Magnifies both gains and losses
   - Can lead to margin calls during market stress
   - May force forced liquidations at unfavorable prices
   - Can cause fund failure if losses exceed capital

2. **Liquidity Risk**: Hedge funds may have liquidity mismatches:
   - Investor lock-ups and gates restrict redemptions
   - Underlying positions may be illiquid
   - Market stress can reduce liquidity of normally liquid assets
   - Creates risk that investors cannot access capital when needed

**(b) Risk Management Technique:**

**Position Limits and Concentration Controls**: Establishing maximum position sizes, sector limits, and concentration guidelines helps prevent overexposure to single positions, sectors, or strategies. This:
- Reduces impact of individual position failures
- Improves portfolio diversification
- Limits leverage exposure to any single risk
- Helps maintain liquidity by avoiding over-concentration in illiquid positions
- Provides early warning if positions grow too large
